Sir Mark Tully, the acclaimed British journalist known for his extensive and insightful reporting on India for the BBC, passed away at the age of 88 on Friday, December 21, 2024, at his residence in Delhi. His death prompted an outpouring of tributes, with Prime Minister Narendra Modi leading the condolences, hailing Tully as "a towering voice of journalism."

In a statement shared on social media, Prime Minister Modi expressed his grief, stating, "Sir Mark Tully will be remembered as a towering voice of journalism, who was deeply connected to India. His decades-long career with the BBC, covering crucial moments and events, left a lasting impact. Pained by his demise. Condolences to his family and admirers." This sentiment underscored the significant relationship Tully cultivated with India over more than three decades.

Tully's career with the British Broadcasting Corporation spanned over 30 years, with a substantial portion dedicated to reporting from India. He served as the BBC's Bureau Chief in Delhi for over 20 years, from 1972 until 1994, becoming one of the most recognized foreign correspondents in the country. During his tenure, he became a familiar voice and face to millions globally, delivering nuanced reports on India's complex social, political, and cultural landscape.

His reporting covered some of India's most defining moments in the latter half of the 20th century. Tully provided firsthand accounts of critical events such as the Emergency (1975-1977), the anti-Sikh riots of 1984 following Operation Blue Star and the assassination of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, and the demolition of the Babri Masjid in 1992. His ability to explain these intricate situations to an international audience, often with a deep understanding of local context, earned him widespread respect.

Beyond his journalistic assignments, Tully was recognized for his profound affection for India, choosing to live in the country for the majority of his adult life even after retiring from the BBC. He was honored by both the Indian and British governments for his contributions. The Government of India awarded him the Padma Shri in 1992 and the Padma Bhushan in 2005, two of the nation's highest civilian awards. In the United Kingdom, he was knighted in 2002, receiving the title Knight Bachelor for his services to broadcasting.

*   **Key Career Highlights:**
    *   Joined BBC in 1964.
    *   Served as BBC Delhi Bureau Chief from 1972 to 1994.
    *   Covered major historical events including the Emergency, Operation Blue Star, and the Babri Masjid demolition.
    *   Authored several books, including "No Full Stops in India" (1991) and "Amritsar: Mrs. Gandhi's Last Battle" (1985).

Following his departure from the BBC, Sir Mark continued to contribute to journalism and literature. He became a prolific author, with his books often exploring various facets of Indian life and society. His distinctive voice and insightful analysis remained influential, shaping perceptions of India globally and fostering a deeper understanding between cultures.

Sir Mark Tully's legacy is marked by his unwavering commitment to objective reporting, his deep empathy for the people and stories of India, and his ability to bridge cultural divides through compelling narratives. His passing marks the end of an era for many who followed his reporting, leaving an indelible mark on both Indian and international journalism.
