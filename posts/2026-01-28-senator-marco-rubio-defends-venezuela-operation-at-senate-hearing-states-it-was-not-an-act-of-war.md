WASHINGTON D.C. – Senator Marco Rubio (R-FL) clarified the nature of a recent operation involving Venezuela during a U.S. Senate hearing, asserting that the action did not constitute an "act of war." His remarks were made to members of Congress and other officials present, focusing on distinguishing the operation from overt military aggression.

Senator Rubio, a vocal figure on U.S. foreign policy toward Latin America, specifically addressed concerns regarding the legal and international implications of U.S. involvement in Venezuela. He stated, "This was not an act of war," aiming to frame the operation within specific parameters that avoid escalation or misinterpretation under international law. The precise details of the operation were not extensively disclosed during the public portion of the hearing, maintaining a focus on the classification of the action rather than its specifics.

The Senator's comments underscore the delicate balance in U.S.-Venezuela relations, which have been characterized by significant diplomatic strain, economic sanctions, and consistent U.S. support for democratic institutions and opposition figures in the country. The U.S. government has, for years, recognized Juan Guaidó as the legitimate interim president of Venezuela, challenging the legitimacy of Nicolás Maduro's administration. This political stance has frequently led to various forms of U.S. engagement, including humanitarian aid initiatives and intelligence gathering, alongside punitive measures against the Maduro regime.

Rubio's defense highlights the distinction between different types of state actions under international law. An "act of war" carries specific legal consequences, potentially invoking self-defense clauses for targeted nations and raising the stakes for international intervention. By categorizing the Venezuela operation otherwise, Rubio sought to manage expectations regarding its scope and intent.

*   **Classification:** The primary assertion was that the operation did not meet the definition of an "act of war."
