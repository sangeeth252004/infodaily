Sneha Singh, an Indian student graduating from the University of California San Diego (UCSD), has reported that her parents were denied B2 tourist visas by the U.S. Embassy in New Delhi. This denial prevented them from attending her commencement ceremony, a significant milestone for international students and their families.

Ms. Singh's parents applied for the B2 non-immigrant visas with the explicit purpose of witnessing their daughter's university graduation. According to Ms. Singh, the visa interview process at the U.S. Embassy in New Delhi was brief, reportedly lasting less than two minutes. During this period, her parents were asked about the purpose of their visit, their daughter's academic program, and their income. Following these questions, the consular officer denied the visas.

The denial was reportedly based on Section 214(b) of the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA). This section states that every alien is presumed to be an immigrant until they establish to the satisfaction of the consular officer that they are entitled to a non-immigrant status. Applicants for B2 visas must demonstrate strong ties to their home country and a clear intent to return after a temporary visit to overcome this presumption.

The inability of her parents to attend the graduation ceremony has been a source of disappointment for Ms. Singh. She expressed that studying abroad is a challenging endeavor, and sharing such a momentous occasion with family holds deep personal significance. This incident highlights a recurring issue faced by some international students whose families encounter difficulties in obtaining visitor visas for key life events like graduations.

Key details of the situation include:
*   **Student:** Sneha Singh
*   **University:** University of California San Diego (UCSD)
*   **Visa Type Applied:** B2 (tourist visa)
*   **Location of Visa Application:** U.S. Embassy/Consulate, New Delhi, India
*   **Reported Reason for Denial:** Section 214(b) of the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), indicating a failure to overcome the presumption of immigrant intent.

While U.S. visa policies are designed to ensure national security and prevent illegal immigration, the process can present challenges for legitimate visitors. The U.S. State Department maintains that visa decisions are made on a case-by-case basis, considering all available information and the specific circumstances of each applicant. Ms. Singh's experience underscores the ongoing complexity and emotional impact of visa application outcomes for international families seeking to participate in their children's academic achievements abroad.
