A significant environmental disaster unfolded on Scotland's River Spey in mid-May, resulting in the widespread death of fish and birds following a suspected chemical spill. The pollution event has affected an approximate 10-mile stretch of the river, located in the Moray region, prompting an urgent investigation by environmental authorities focusing on a potential industrial source.

The Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA) and the Spey Fishery Board confirmed a serious pollution incident, initially discovered around May 15. Hundreds of salmon and trout, critical to the river's ecosystem and local economy, have been found deceased. Additionally, various bird species, including gulls, ducks, and dippers, have succumbed to the contamination.

The River Spey is renowned as one of Scotland's most significant salmon fishing rivers, supporting a substantial tourism industry and local employment. The extent of the ecological damage has raised considerable concern among environmentalists, local residents, and the fishing community. Initial observations and testing indicate the presence of a chemical pollutant, though its specific nature has not been publicly disclosed by investigators.

Key details regarding the incident include:
*   **Location:** A roughly 10-mile section of the River Spey, primarily between Rothes and areas upstream, within Moray, Scotland.
*   **Timeline:** The pollution was first reported and observed in mid-May, with fish and bird deaths continuing to be discovered over the subsequent days.
*   **Affected Wildlife:** Hundreds of fish, predominantly salmon and trout, have been confirmed dead. Bird species observed among the casualties include gulls, ducks, and dippers.
*   **Cause:** A suspected chemical spill is believed to be the root cause. Investigations are actively targeting an industrial facility operating near the affected stretch of the river as a potential source.
*   **Authorities Involved:** SEPA is leading the environmental investigation, working in close cooperation with the Spey Fishery Board.

The immediate impact extends beyond wildlife mortality. Public health warnings have been issued, advising local residents and visitors to avoid contact with the river water in the affected area and to refrain from consuming any dead fish or birds recovered from the river. This precautionary measure aims to prevent potential health risks associated with unknown chemical contaminants.

SEPA has initiated a comprehensive investigation, deploying specialist teams to collect samples and trace the source of the pollution. The agency has not yet named any specific industrial facility but has confirmed that their inquiries are focused on identifying the party responsible for the discharge. The investigation involves analyzing water samples, examining the remains of deceased wildlife, and reviewing industrial operations in the vicinity.

The long-term environmental consequences for the River Spey remain a critical concern. Restoration efforts may be extensive and prolonged, particularly given the river's ecological significance and the time required for fish populations to recover. Further updates from SEPA are anticipated as the investigation progresses, with potential enforcement actions to follow if a polluter is identified.
