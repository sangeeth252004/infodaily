TTV Dhinakaran has issued a stern warning to expelled Member of Legislative Assembly (MLA) S. Kamaraj, indicating a move to initiate disqualification proceedings against him. The warning comes in the wake of Kamaraj's decision to support the TVK during a recent floor test, an action that has ignited a political row.

The dispute centers on the implications of an MLA, already expelled from their original party, aligning with another political entity during a crucial legislative confidence vote. Dhinakaran's statement suggests his faction views Kamaraj's support for the TVK as a direct violation of legislative conduct and anti-defection principles, potentially warranting the forfeiture of his assembly seat.

*   **Key Figures:** The primary individuals involved are TTV Dhinakaran, a prominent political leader, and S. Kamaraj, an MLA who had previously been expelled from Dhinakaran's political outfit. The TVK is the political group that Kamaraj reportedly supported during the floor test.
*   **Event Context:** A floor test is a legislative procedure used to determine if a government or a specific political leader still commands the confidence of the legislative assembly. An MLA's vote in such a test is critical, and any deviation from party lines, especially by an expelled member still holding a seat, can trigger significant political repercussions.

The core of the matter lies in India's anti-defection law, enshrined in the Tenth Schedule of the Constitution. This law aims to prevent political defections and maintain party discipline by penalizing legislators who switch parties or defy their party's whip on specific votes. While S. Kamaraj was already expelled, the question of his legislative seat remains under the purview of this law if he is deemed to have voluntarily given up membership of his original political party or voted contrary to its directives, even if expelled. An MLA holds their seat based on the party symbol under which they were elected, and their expulsion does not automatically mean disqualification from the assembly.

Dhinakaran's warning signals an intent to formally petition the Speaker of the legislative assembly for Kamaraj's disqualification. The Speaker, as the adjudicating authority under the anti-defection law, would then be tasked with hearing arguments from both sides before rendering a decision. Such proceedings can be time-consuming and often lead to legal challenges in higher courts.

The political ramifications of such a disqualification, if it proceeds, could be significant. It would reduce the numerical strength of MLAs in the assembly, potentially impacting future legislative votes and the balance of power. For Dhinakaran's faction, it represents an assertion of party discipline and a clear message against cross-party support by former members. For S. Kamaraj, it initiates a battle to retain his legislative position.

The immediate next step is anticipated to be the filing of a formal petition by Dhinakaran or his representatives with the Speaker's office, detailing the grounds for disqualification based on Kamaraj's actions during the floor test. This will initiate a formal legal and political process, with the Speaker's ruling being a critical juncture for both parties.
