The Indian government, through Additional Solicitor General S.V. Raju, recently presented arguments before the Supreme Court regarding the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) bail provisions, particularly in cases of prolonged trial delays. During a recent hearing, Mr. Raju posed a hypothetical question concerning whether a perpetrator like Ajmal Kasab would be granted bail if his trial faced significant delays, thereby emphasizing the grave national security implications of UAPA offenses.

This submission comes as the Supreme Court considers the scope of bail in UAPA cases, especially after its July 2023 judgment in the *Vernon Gonsalves* and *Arun Ferreira* cases. That ruling indicated that prolonged incarceration without a speedy trial could be a factor for granting bail, thereby refining the interpretation of Section 43D(5) of the UAPA, which places stringent conditions on bail for individuals accused of terror-related activities.

Additional Solicitor General Raju contended that offenses under the UAPA, which deal with terrorism, national security, and territorial integrity, cannot be equated with ordinary criminal cases. He argued that the nature of these crimes necessitates stricter bail norms, even in the face of trial delays. The government's position is that granting bail solely on the grounds of delay could compromise national security, impede ongoing investigations into serious terror plots, and pose a threat to public safety.

The reference to Ajmal Kasab, the sole terrorist captured alive during the 26/11 Mumbai attacks, was used rhetorically to illustrate potential ramifications if bail provisions for terror suspects were to be significantly relaxed. Kasab was tried and convicted under the Indian Penal Code and other specific anti-terror laws, not primarily the UAPA. Mr. Raju's point was to highlight the severe threat posed by such individuals and the perceived risks to national security if they were released pending trial due to procedural delays. The government’s argument underscored the unique challenges in prosecuting complex terror cases, which often involve extensive evidence collection, international cooperation, and multiple accused.

Further concerns raised by the government included the potential for accused individuals in UAPA cases to abscond, tamper with evidence, or intimidate witnesses if released on bail. Such actions, they argued, could severely undermine the justice delivery system and the effectiveness of anti-terror efforts. The government asserted that the primary consideration in UAPA cases must remain national security and the prevention of further terrorist activities, balancing it with the accused's right to a speedy trial.

The Supreme Court had previously emphasized the right to a speedy trial as a fundamental aspect of justice. The current debate seeks to define the appropriate balance between this constitutional right and the unique challenges associated with prosecuting UAPA cases and ensuring national security.

The Supreme Court continues to hear arguments on this critical legal issue, which holds significant implications for the interpretation of UAPA, the rights of the accused, and the government's ability to combat terrorism effectively. The outcome of these proceedings is anticipated to further define the parameters for granting bail in terror-related cases across India.
