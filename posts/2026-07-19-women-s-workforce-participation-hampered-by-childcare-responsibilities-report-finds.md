A recent report by the International Labour Organization (ILO), supported by data from India's National Sample Survey Office (NSO), indicates that unpaid care work, particularly childcare responsibilities, remains a significant barrier preventing many women from participating in the formal workforce. The findings underscore a critical challenge for economic development and gender equality, with India's female labour force participation rate (FLFPR) recorded at 37% in 2022-23.

The ILO report, titled 'Care at Work', details how the disproportionate burden of caregiving on women directly impacts their ability to seek, retain, or return to employment. This phenomenon contributes to lost productivity and slower economic growth, as a substantial portion of the potential workforce remains underutilised. The NSO data further corroborates this trend, showing consistent patterns of women withdrawing from or not entering the labour market due to family and care obligations.

Key factors contributing to this challenge include:
*   **Lack of Accessible Childcare:** A shortage of affordable, high-quality childcare facilities forces many women to assume full-time caregiving roles.
*   **High Cost of Private Care:** Where private childcare options exist, their prohibitive cost makes them inaccessible for a large segment of the population.
*   **Limited Employer Support:** Many workplaces do not offer sufficient support mechanisms, such as on-site crèches or flexible work arrangements, to assist working parents.
*   **Societal Norms:** Persistent traditional gender roles often place the primary responsibility for childcare and household duties on women, influencing their career trajectories.

Experts cited within the report emphasize that addressing these systemic issues is crucial for enhancing women's economic empowerment. The current situation not only limits individual women's opportunities but also restricts the overall economic potential of the nation. The findings highlight the necessity for comprehensive policy interventions to create a more equitable environment for women in the workforce.

Recommendations put forth by the ILO and other stakeholders include:
*   **Investment in Care Services:** Increased public and private investment in a robust network of affordable and quality childcare facilities.
*   **Promotion of Parental Leave:** Implementation and enforcement of comprehensive parental leave policies that encourage shared care responsibilities between parents.
*   **Flexible Work Arrangements:** Encouraging employers to adopt flexible working hours, remote work options, and other arrangements that support work-life balance for caregivers.
*   **Tackling Gender Stereotypes:** Public awareness campaigns and educational initiatives aimed at challenging and changing traditional gender stereotypes related to care work.

The sustained low FLFPR due to these factors represents an ongoing concern for policymakers. Moving forward, a concerted effort across government, industry, and civil society is anticipated to address the structural and societal impediments that keep women outside the workforce, with the aim of fostering inclusive economic growth and achieving greater gender parity.
