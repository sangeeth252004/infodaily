Ongoing maritime disruptions in the Red Sea region are creating new logistical challenges for India's crucial crude oil imports, particularly impacting the supply of Russian crude. Since late 2023, attacks on commercial shipping in the vital waterway have prompted many shipping firms to re-route vessels, bypassing the Suez Canal and instead navigating around the Cape of Good Hope. This diversion directly affects the established supply chains for Russian oil destined for Indian refineries.

India emerged as a primary buyer of Russian crude following international sanctions imposed after February 2022, with imports significantly increasing throughout 2022 and 2023. The Red Sea and Suez Canal route has been instrumental in facilitating this trade, offering the shortest passage between Russian Baltic and Black Sea ports and Indian west coast refineries. The current disruptions necessitate longer voyages, adding substantial time and cost to deliveries.

*   **Extended Transit Times:** Re-routing around Africa's southern tip adds approximately 10 to 15 days to a typical journey from Russian ports to India. This extended duration delays crude deliveries and reduces the number of voyages tankers can complete within a given period.
*   **Increased Freight Costs:** The longer routes consume more fuel and incur higher operational costs. Additionally, war risk insurance premiums for vessels traversing or near the Red Sea have escalated significantly, further inflating overall freight expenses.
*   **Impact on Discounted Crude:** While Russian crude has been offered at discounted rates, the cumulative increase in shipping and insurance costs diminishes the economic advantage for Indian refiners. This dynamic could compel Indian buyers to re-evaluate procurement strategies.
*   **Vessel Availability:** The stretched transit times also tie up crude tankers for longer periods, potentially tightening vessel availability for future shipments and contributing to higher spot freight rates.

Industry analysis indicates that these logistical pressures could contribute to upward movement in landed crude oil prices for India, despite continued discounts on the crude itself. India's reliance on seaborne crude imports, which meet over 80% of its domestic demand, makes it particularly vulnerable to sustained disruptions in major shipping arteries.

While the Red Sea situation affects global shipping and crude flows from various regions, including Saudi Arabia and other Middle Eastern producers, its specific impact on the Russian crude corridor to India is notable due to the recent strategic shift in India's oil procurement. Indian refiners and government entities continue to monitor the evolving situation closely, assessing potential long-term implications for energy security and domestic fuel prices. The duration and intensity of the Red Sea disruptions remain key factors in determining the broader economic impact on India's energy sector.
