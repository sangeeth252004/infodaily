NEW DELHI – The Supreme Court of India has ordered a fresh trial for six individuals accused in a blast case dating back to 1996, setting aside their convictions primarily on the grounds that they were denied adequate legal assistance during the initial court proceedings. The ruling, delivered by a bench comprising Justices B.R. Gavai and Sandeep Mehta, underscores the fundamental right to legal representation as an indispensable component of a fair trial.

The case, which is nearly three decades old, involved six accused – Bhupinder Singh, Ravinder Kumar, Balwinder Singh, Gurmukh Singh, Jasbir Singh, and Kulwinder Singh. They had been convicted under the Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act (TADA) by a Special TADA Court, a conviction subsequently upheld by the Punjab and Haryana High Court. The Supreme Court's intervention came after observing that the accused had either been unrepresented or inadequately represented by competent counsel, particularly in the absence of state-provided legal aid, which is a constitutional mandate.

The apex court's decision hinges on Articles 21 and 39A of the Indian Constitution. Article 21 guarantees the right to life and personal liberty, which has been interpreted by the judiciary to include the right to a fair trial. Article 39A specifically mandates the state to provide free legal aid to ensure that justice is not denied to any citizen due to economic or other disabilities. The bench highlighted that the right to legal aid is not merely a formality but a substantive right crucial for ensuring equality of arms in criminal proceedings, especially for indigent accused facing serious charges.

*   **Case Origin:** The blast incident occurred in Chandigarh in 1996.
*   **Accused Convicted:** All six individuals were handed life imprisonment sentences.
*   **Prior Judgments:** The convictions were initially pronounced by a Special TADA Court and later affirmed by the Punjab and Haryana High Court.
*   **Supreme Court's Rationale:** The lack of proper legal representation for the accused at critical stages of the trial process was deemed a violation of their fundamental rights.
*   **Constitutional Basis:** The judgment reiterated the importance of Article 21 (Right to Life and Personal Liberty) and Article 39A (Free Legal Aid).

The Supreme Court's order mandates that the matter be remanded back to the trial court for a complete fresh trial. The trial court is now directed to ensure that the accused are provided with competent legal aid at the expense of the state, thereby facilitating a fair and impartial hearing in strict adherence to due process. This ruling reinforces previous precedents, such as the *Mohd Hussain @ Julfikar Ali vs The State (Govt of NCT of Delhi)* case, which similarly emphasized the right to legal representation.

This judgment carries significant implications for the criminal justice system in India, reaffirming the judiciary's commitment to upholding fundamental rights, particularly for those who lack the means to secure adequate legal representation. It serves as a reminder to the state and its legal aid bodies to proactively ensure that justice is accessible and fair for all, irrespective of their socio-economic status. The fresh trial will now proceed, with the state bound to provide the necessary legal support to the accused, many of whom have already spent considerable time incarcerated.
