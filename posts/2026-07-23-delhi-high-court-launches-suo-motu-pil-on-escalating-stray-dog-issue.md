NEW DELHI – The Delhi High Court has taken suo motu cognizance of the escalating issue of stray dog attacks in the capital, initiating a Public Interest Litigation (PIL) to address the "alarming number of dog bite incidents" reported daily. The court’s action, taken on a recent date, underscores growing concerns over public safety and health risks associated with the increasing stray dog population across Delhi.

Justice Subramonium Prasad, presiding over the matter, emphasized the severity of the situation, noting, "There are bite incidents every day." The court has appointed senior advocate Dayan Krishnan and advocate Aditya Singh as amicus curiae to assist in finding comprehensive solutions to this persistent problem. Their role will involve presenting factual information, legal precedents, and potential remedies to the court, ensuring a balanced approach to both public safety and animal welfare.

The suo motu PIL signifies the court's direct intervention without a formal petition from an affected party, reflecting the perceived urgency and widespread impact of the issue. The court’s decision comes amid continuous reports of individuals, including children, being injured by stray dogs, raising questions about the effectiveness of existing animal control measures and the responsibilities of civic bodies.

Key aspects expected to be addressed by the PIL include:
*   **Effectiveness of Animal Birth Control (ABC) Programs:** An assessment of current sterilization and vaccination drives conducted by municipal corporations and other local authorities.
*   **Infrastructure for Stray Dogs:** Evaluating the availability and adequacy of shelters, adoption centers, and veterinary care facilities.
*   **Public Awareness Campaigns:** The need for initiatives to educate residents on safe interactions with stray animals and responsible pet ownership.
*   **Enforcement of Existing Laws:** Reviewing the implementation of Animal Welfare Board of India guidelines and other relevant regulations concerning stray animals.
*   **Data Collection:** Establishing robust mechanisms for recording dog bite incidents and stray dog populations to inform policy decisions.

The increasing number of stray dogs in urban areas presents a multifaceted challenge, balancing compassionate treatment of animals with the imperative to ensure human safety. Dog bites can lead to serious injuries, infections, and the risk of rabies, a fatal disease if not treated promptly. Public health experts have consistently highlighted the need for robust public health interventions, including widespread anti-rabies vaccination programs for both humans and animals.

Municipal corporations, including the Municipal Corporation of Delhi (MCD) and other local bodies, bear primary responsibility for managing stray animal populations within their jurisdictions. Their efforts typically involve Animal Birth Control (ABC) programs, which aim to humanely reduce the stray population through sterilization and vaccination, followed by the release of the dogs back into their original territories as per legal mandates. However, the efficacy and scale of these programs have often been debated, particularly in rapidly expanding urban centers like Delhi.

The Delhi High Court's intervention signals a judicial push for more coordinated and effective strategies. The appointed amicus curiae are expected to engage with various stakeholders, including civic authorities, animal welfare organizations, and public health experts, to gather comprehensive data and propose actionable recommendations. Further proceedings in the PIL are anticipated to involve detailed reports and submissions from these parties, laying the groundwork for potential policy changes and enhanced enforcement mechanisms. The court’s focus remains on establishing a sustainable framework to mitigate the risks posed by stray dogs while upholding animal welfare principles.
