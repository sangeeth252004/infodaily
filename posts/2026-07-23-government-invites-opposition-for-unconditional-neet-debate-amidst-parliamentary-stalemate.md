NEW DELHI – The Indian government has extended an invitation to opposition leaders for a debate on the NEET-UG 2024 examination controversy, urging them to engage in discussions without imposing preconditions, such as demands for ministerial resignations. Parliamentary Affairs Minister Kiren Rijiju made the overture, emphasizing the government's readiness for a comprehensive discussion to resolve the parliamentary deadlock that has characterized the initial days of the current session.

The invitation comes amidst widespread protests and persistent demands from opposition parties for a detailed investigation into alleged irregularities surrounding the National Eligibility-cum-Entrance Test (Undergraduate) 2024. The examination, conducted by the National Testing Agency (NTA), has faced scrutiny over alleged paper leaks, discrepancies in grace marks, and other administrative issues, prompting calls for its re-examination and the dissolution of the NTA.

Minister Rijiju, addressing the media, stated that the government is fully prepared to provide a "point-by-point response" to all concerns raised by the opposition regarding the NEET issue. He underscored the importance of resuming normal parliamentary proceedings, asserting that the government's position is to allow legislative business to continue while simultaneously addressing pressing national concerns. "We want to discuss everything; the government is not running away from any debate. But there should be no conditions like resignations before we even begin to talk," Rijiju said, reiterating the stance against what he described as obstructive tactics.

The parliamentary session has seen repeated adjournments due to vociferous protests from opposition benches, demanding accountability from the government regarding the integrity of competitive examinations. Key demands from the opposition include:
*   A Supreme Court-monitored investigation into the NEET-UG 2024 alleged irregularities.
*   The immediate dissolution of the National Testing Agency (NTA).
*   Accountability from Union Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan.
*   Assurances for the future integrity of national examinations.

The government, while acknowledging the seriousness of the allegations and initiating steps like transferring the investigation to the Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI) and forming a high-level committee for NTA reforms, has maintained that discussions should proceed in an orderly parliamentary fashion. The education ministry has also confirmed the cancellation of the UGC-NET examination and postponed the CSIR-NET in light of potential irregularities.

The current deadlock prevents critical legislative work and important policy discussions. The government's latest outreach signals an attempt to break the impasse and encourage constructive dialogue. The onus now rests with the opposition parties to respond to the invitation, determining whether the parliamentary proceedings will shift from disruption to debate in the coming days. The resolution of this issue remains a focal point for millions of students and their families nationwide.
