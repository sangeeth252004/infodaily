A U.S. federal immigration agency has put forth a new proposal that could enable the denial of work permits to non-citizens based on any prior arrest, irrespective of whether the arrest led to a conviction. This potential policy shift marks a significant departure from existing practices, which typically consider criminal convictions rather than mere arrests as grounds for such denials. Immigration lawyers and advocacy groups are urging caution, highlighting the broad implications for individuals seeking to live and work in the United States.

The proposed rule, detailed in an official announcement, grants adjudicating officers expanded discretion to consider an individual's arrest history as a disqualifying factor for work authorization. Currently, U.S. immigration law generally focuses on convictions for serious offenses or specific criminal acts when evaluating eligibility for immigration benefits. The new framework would lower this threshold considerably, potentially impacting a wide range of applicants, from those with minor past infractions to individuals who were arrested but never charged, or whose cases were dismissed.

Key details of the proposal include:
*   **Broad Scope:** The term "any arrest" suggests a comprehensive application, encompassing various types of law enforcement encounters.
*   **Discretionary Power:** Adjudicators would gain greater authority to weigh arrest records when evaluating work permit applications.
*   **Shift from Conviction Standard:** The proposal moves away from the established principle that an individual is presumed innocent until proven guilty, by allowing arrests alone to trigger adverse immigration consequences.

Immigration legal professionals have voiced strong concerns regarding the proposal's potential impact on due process and fairness. They argue that an arrest does not equate to guilt and that relying on arrest records alone could unfairly penalize individuals, including those who were wrongfully arrested or those whose charges were dropped or dismissed. Legal experts point out that even minor incidents, such as civil disputes or mistaken identity, can lead to an arrest, and under this proposal, such events could have severe, lasting immigration consequences. This broad scope raises questions about how the policy would distinguish between serious threats and minor incidents, or even politically motivated arrests.

The policy also presents challenges for transparency and consistency in decision-making. Without clear guidelines on how adjudicators should weigh different types of arrests, there is a risk of arbitrary denials and inconsistent application across cases. This uncertainty could create significant hurdles for foreign nationals, including skilled workers, asylum seekers, and family members of U.S. citizens, who are applying for the ability to work legally.

This proposal is expected to undergo a period of public comment, allowing stakeholders, legal experts, and the general public to submit feedback. The federal agency responsible will then review these comments before potentially finalizing and implementing the rule. Legal and advocacy organizations are preparing to submit detailed responses, emphasizing the need for policies that uphold due process and avoid creating undue barriers for non-citizens contributing to the U.S. economy and society. The timeline for a final decision remains subject to the administrative process.
