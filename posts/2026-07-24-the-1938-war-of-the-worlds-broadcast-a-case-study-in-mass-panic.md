On October 30, 1938, a radio drama broadcast across the United States by Orson Welles and the Mercury Theatre on the Air led to widespread public alarm, as many listeners mistook the fictional adaptation of H.G. Wells' "The War of the Worlds" for real news bulletins reporting an alien invasion. The CBS Radio Network broadcast, simulating a series of urgent news interruptions detailing an invasion from Mars, prompted calls to police, newspaper offices, and radio stations from concerned citizens.

The hour-long broadcast, which began at 8:00 PM Eastern Standard Time, was designed to sound like a typical Sunday evening program featuring a dance band performance, periodically interrupted by increasingly alarming news flashes. These simulated reports detailed strange explosions on Mars, a meteor shower in Grover's Mill, New Jersey, and the subsequent emergence of hostile alien invaders equipped with heat ray weapons. The innovative use of realism, including sound effects and the authoritative tone of broadcast journalists, contributed significantly to the audience's misinterpretation.

Many listeners tuned in late, missing the program's opening announcement that it was a dramatization. This segment of the audience, hearing only the "news bulletins" out of context, believed they were experiencing live reporting of an actual extraterrestrial attack. While precise figures are debated, estimates suggest a significant portion of the estimated six million listeners believed the broadcast to be real.

Key aspects of the broadcast and its immediate aftermath include:
*   **Date:** Sunday, October 30, 1938
*   **Broadcaster:** CBS Radio Network
*   **Program:** The Mercury Theatre on the Air, directed and narrated by Orson Welles.
*   **Format:** Fictional news bulletins, interviews with "experts," and on-the-scene reporting.
*   **Public Reaction:** Reports of people fleeing their homes, attempting to warn neighbors, and jamming phone lines. Police stations and emergency services received numerous calls about the unfolding "events."
*   **Geographic Impact:** Panic was concentrated in areas where the fictional events were said to be occurring, such as Grover's Mill, New Jersey, and parts of New York.

The event quickly became a national phenomenon, making front-page news across the country on October 31. Newspapers reported on the panic, featuring accounts of individuals who were genuinely terrified. Orson Welles and CBS faced public backlash, with some critics accusing them of irresponsible broadcasting. Welles publicly apologized, stating that the intention was purely entertainment and that they had not anticipated such a widespread reaction.

The "War of the Worlds" broadcast served as a critical case study in the nascent field of mass communication and media literacy. It highlighted the profound power of radio, then a dominant medium, to influence public perception and behavior. The incident prompted extensive research into listener psychology and the effects of media on audiences. It also led to discussions about the ethical responsibilities of broadcasters and the need for clear differentiation between factual reporting and fictional entertainment. Regulations and guidelines regarding simulated news bulletins in dramatic programming were subsequently reviewed by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC). The event remains a notable example in media history, illustrating the impact of innovative storytelling and the complexities of public information dissemination.
