### Augmented Reality (AR)

Augmented reality integrates computer-generated elements, such as images, sounds, or text, with the user's actual surroundings. This technology typically uses devices like smartphones, tablets, or AR glasses to display these digital overlays. The real world remains visible and accessible, with the digital content acting as an enhancement or addition.

**Example:** A mobile app that allows you to see how a piece of furniture would look in your living room by placing a 3D model of the furniture onto the live camera feed of your room.

### Virtual Reality (VR)

Virtual reality creates a completely artificial, computer-generated environment that users can explore and interact with. This experience requires a VR headset, which covers the user's eyes and ears, effectively blocking out the physical world. The goal is to foster a sense of presence within the simulated space.

**Example:** Playing a video game where the headset transports you to a fantasy world, allowing you to look around and move within that digital landscape.

### Key Differentiating Factors

The fundamental difference is the relationship with the user's physical environment. AR enhances reality, while VR replaces it. This leads to different hardware requirements and distinct user experiences. AR often requires less intrusive technology, allowing for greater interaction with the immediate surroundings, whereas VR aims for total immersion.

### Limitations and Edge Cases

Both technologies have limitations. AR can sometimes struggle with accurate depth perception or real-time environmental mapping. VR can cause motion sickness for some users, and the isolation from the real world can be a safety concern if not managed properly. The fidelity of the digital overlays or the realism of the virtual environments are also ongoing areas of development for both.
